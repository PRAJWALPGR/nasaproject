Overview of Phytoplankton

Phytoplankton are microscopic organisms that inhabit the upper layers of the oceans, lakes, and rivers. They are crucial as primary producers, converting sunlight and carbon dioxide into organic matter through the process of photosynthesis. This unique ability allows phytoplankton to contribute approximately 50% of the Earth’s oxygen, making them essential not only for marine life but also for life on land.

Phytoplankton are divided into two major groups: diatoms and dinoflagellates. Diatoms have intricate silica cell walls and are commonly found in nutrient-rich waters, where they thrive. Their silica shells can accumulate on the ocean floor, forming sedimentary deposits known as diatomaceous earth, which have various industrial uses. On the other hand, dinoflagellates are characterized by their flagella, enabling movement through the water. Some species can exhibit bioluminescence, creating stunning light displays in the ocean at night.

These organisms play a critical role in the marine food web, serving as a primary food source for zooplankton, which are then consumed by larger fish, marine mammals, and other predators. As a fundamental component of oceanic ecosystems, phytoplankton significantly influence nutrient cycling and energy flow within marine environments.

Phytoplankton populations are highly sensitive to environmental changes, including temperature fluctuations, nutrient availability, and light conditions. Consequently, shifts in their populations can have profound effects on marine ecosystems. For instance, during periods of nutrient enrichment—often due to runoff from agricultural practices—phytoplankton blooms can occur. While these blooms can boost productivity temporarily, they can also lead to hypoxic conditions (commonly known as dead zones) when the phytoplankton die and decompose, consuming oxygen in the process.

Research into phytoplankton is essential for understanding climate change as they play a significant role in carbon sequestration. By absorbing carbon dioxide during photosynthesis, they help regulate atmospheric CO2 levels. When phytoplankton die, some of their biomass sinks to the ocean floor, effectively trapping carbon for long periods. This process—known as the biological carbon pump—is vital for mitigating climate change and understanding future ocean health.

Sargassum

Mats of this floating seaweed can serve as oases for diverse ecosystems in otherwise desolate parts of our ocean. Named hundreds of years ago by the crew of Christopher Columbus, Sargassum has recently become a nuisance along many coasts. Huge quantities have been washing ashore, piling up and rotting on beaches. This has impeded normal fishing activities and driven away tourists.

Sargassum reflects strongly in the yellow and orange parts of the visible color spectrum. This is seen in a plot of Reflectance vs. Wavelength for five samples (Hu, 2022).

Microcystis

Found in freshwater, Microcystis can form colonies that are bound by a thick mucilage (a.k.a. "goo"). Colonies can become large floating blooms by producing gas-filled bubbles. If consumed by mammals - including humans - Microcystis can cause numbness, nausea, dizziness, vomiting, and lead to liver damage or death.

Microcystis reflects in the green-yellow part of the visible color spectrum. This is seen in a plot of Reflectance vs. Wavelength for three samples (Hu, 2022)

Red Noctiluca

Microscopic algae known as Noctiluca are among the most abundant "red tide" organisms. Although this red-colored species looks scary (unless you really love tomato soup), it is non-toxic. However, large blooms – which occur worldwide – can be harmful when they die and deplete oxygen from ocean habitats.

This species of Noctiluca reflects strongly in the orange and red parts of the visible color spectrum. This is seen in a plot of Reflectance vs. Wavelength for four samples (Hu, 2022).

Trichodesmium

Trichodesmium is one of the few phytoplankton species that can be seen with the naked eye. How? As they age, these relatively large cells become positively buoyant and can aggregate into large floating mats of thread-like filaments. This warm-water species can take nitrogen gas from the atmosphere and convert into useful forms, benefiting other members of the food web.

Trichodesmium has a distinct feature known as "spectral wiggling." These wiggles occur from the blue to the orange portion of the visible spectrum.

Importance of Phytoplankton

Phytoplankton are not just vital for marine ecosystems; they also play a crucial role in global ecological balance. As primary producers at the base of the aquatic food web, they support a wide range of marine life including fish and larger predators such as seals and whales.

Their ability to convert sunlight into energy through photosynthesis makes them essential for oxygen production—contributing up to half of the Earth's oxygen supply. This process not only supports marine life but also sustains terrestrial life forms that rely on oxygen for survival.

Moreover, phytoplankton influence global climate patterns by regulating atmospheric carbon dioxide levels. They absorb CO2 during photosynthesis and release oxygen back into the atmosphere. The carbon that is not consumed by other organisms eventually sinks to the ocean floor when phytoplankton die—a process that effectively sequesters carbon for long periods.

This biological carbon pump is essential in mitigating climate change effects by reducing greenhouse gases in our atmosphere. Understanding phytoplankton dynamics is therefore critical for predicting future climate scenarios and developing strategies for climate change mitigation.

Additionally, phytoplankton contribute to nutrient cycling within marine environments. They help recycle nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus through their life cycles—nutrients that are vital for sustaining marine ecosystems.

Aerosols and Climate

Aerosols are tiny particles or droplets suspended in the atmosphere that can affect climate by scattering and absorbing sunlight. They originate from both natural sources (like volcanic eruptions and sea spray) and human activities (such as industrial emissions). Understanding aerosols is crucial because they play a significant role in cloud formation and climate regulation.

Aerosols influence cloud properties by acting as cloud condensation nuclei (CCN), which help droplets form within clouds. This interaction affects cloud reflectivity (albedo), lifetime, and precipitation patterns—ultimately influencing weather systems globally.

The relationship between aerosols and phytoplankton is particularly interesting; aerosols can deposit nutrients like iron into oceans which may stimulate phytoplankton growth in nutrient-poor areas. However, excessive aerosol pollution can lead to negative consequences such as reduced sunlight penetration into water bodies—affecting photosynthesis rates among phytoplankton.

The impact of aerosols on climate is complex; while some aerosols cool the Earth by reflecting sunlight away from it (like sulfate aerosols), others contribute to warming by absorbing heat (like black carbon). This duality complicates our understanding of climate change dynamics.

AEROSALS:

Sea Spray

These naturally occurring aerosols are generated by the action of ocean waves, which propel salt particles into the air. When lifted high aloft, clouds can condense around sea salt aerosols. Thus, as one of the most abundant aerosol types over the global ocean, sea salt plays an important role in Earth's climate.

The reflectance of sea spray is very low over the range of wavelengths.This is seen in this plot of Reflectance vs. Wavelength from Sayer et al. (2012).

CLOUD

Predicting climate involves long-term studies of clouds in Earth's atmosphere, which both reflect and absorb sunlight. Clouds interact with tiny suspended particles known as aerosols. Aerosols and clouds can interact in complex ways, which are not well understood. For example, cloud drops can form on aerosols and aerosols can be washed out of the air by rain.

Cloud spectra have many "ups and downs" because of variation in absorption among different forms of water: liquid, vapor, and ice. This is seen in a plot of Reflectance vs. Wavelength from data based on Dierssen (2019).

Smoke

Wildfires are unplanned fires that burn in natural areas like forests, grasslands or prairies. As wildfires burn through fuels such as dry forests, large amounts of fine particulate matter are released into the atmosphere and mix with gases to become aerosols, tiny particles suspended in our atmosphere.

Smoke’s spectral signature has a distinct downward slope moving from the visible colors to longer wavelengths. This slope is seen from left to right in a plot of Reflectance vs. Wavelength from Sayer et al. (2014).

Dust

Dust can travel thousands of kilometers – even across ocean basins. Wind-blown dust can be a blessing for some species and a curse for others. Organisms in the ocean located far from rivers can use the nutrients delivered by dust to grow. On the other hand, humans and animals can suffer respiratory issues when dust particles are inhaled.

The orange-red color commonly associated with dust is because it tends to preferentially absorb blue light. This is seen in a plot of Reflectance vs. Wavelength from data based on Lee et al. (2017) and Hess et al. (1998).

Data Utilization

The data collected by NASA's PACE mission will be invaluable across various sectors including fisheries management, climate research, and public health monitoring. By providing comprehensive data on phytoplankton distributions and health metrics alongside aerosol properties and cloud dynamics, PACE aims to enhance our understanding of oceanic processes.

Fisheries management relies heavily on understanding phytoplankton dynamics since these organisms form the base of aquatic food webs. Monitoring changes in phytoplankton populations helps predict fish stocks' health and sustainability—critical information for maintaining fisheries worldwide.

In climate research contexts, PACE data will provide insights into how changes in oceanic conditions affect global carbon cycling processes. Understanding these interactions is vital for developing effective climate models that predict future scenarios under different greenhouse gas emission pathways.

Public health implications arise from understanding how aerosols affect air quality and respiratory health issues in humans. PACE's ability to monitor aerosol concentrations will inform policies aimed at improving air quality standards—ensuring healthier environments for communities globally.

About Plankton, Aerosol, Cloud, Ocean Ecosystem (PACE)

Why Do We Need PACE?

Ocean Ecology:

Our ocean teems with life and many of its most vital species are invisible to us. Like on land, the ocean has deserts, forests, meadows, and jungles, providing habitats for many forms of life. The types of life in these habitats is determined by microscopic algae that float in our ocean. Known as "phytoplankton," these tiny organisms come in many different shapes, sizes, and colors. The diversity of phytoplankton types determines the roles they play in ocean habitats. It also determines how well they capture energy from the sun and carbon from the atmosphere.

Phytoplankton provide food for small zooplankton, tiny animals that float in our ocean. Like humans, these grazers actively select their food. In the same way, larger zooplankton prey upon smaller zooplankton. Step by step, energy captured from phytoplankton transfers to bigger creatures. As the energy climbs the marine food web, it can ultimately be used by humans.

The ocean is a fluid that is constantly in motion. Hosting the largest three-dimensional living space on earth, it supports many habitats. For example, the North Atlantic is home to highly productive "forests" each spring. Its blooms of carbon-rich phytoplankton fuel the fisheries of New England. The crystal-clear waters around Florida host productive coral reefs and fisheries. At times, however, this area is plagued by toxic phytoplankton.

Today's satellites reveal the quantity of phytoplankton at the ocean surface. Yet we cannot detect the diversity of species. For the first time, PACE's unprecedented technology will:

  • Reveal the diversity of phytoplankton found in our ocean on global scales;
  • Allow us to understand the role that phytoplankton diversity has on life in the ocean
  • Help us predict the "boom or bust" of fisheries along with marine hazards such as harmful algal blooms.

What is PACE?

PACE is NASA's Plankton, Aerosol, Cloud, ocean Ecosystem mission, currently in the design phase of mission development. It is scheduled to launch in 2024, extending and improving NASA's over 20-year record of satellite observations of global ocean biology, aerosols (tiny particles suspended in the atmosphere), and clouds.

PACE will advance the assessment of ocean health by measuring the distribution of phytoplankton, tiny plants and algae that sustain the marine food web. It will also continue systematic records of key atmospheric variables associated with air quality and Earth's climate.

PACE has two fundamental science goals:

  • To extend key systematic ocean color, aerosol, and cloud data records for Earth system and climate studies.
  • To address new and emerging science questions using its advanced instruments, surpassing the capabilities of previous and current missions.

The primary science instruments planned for PACE are:

Ocean Color Instrument (OCI) -

Spectrometer used to measure intensity of light over portions of the electromagnetic spectrum: ultraviolet (UV), visible, near infrared, and several shortwave infrared bands. The OCI will enable continuous measurement of light at finer wavelength resolution than previous NASA ocean color sensors, providing detailed information on our global ocean. The color of the ocean is determined by the interaction of sunlight with substances or particles present in seawater such as chlorophyll, a green photosynthetic pigment found in phytoplankton and land plants.

Multi-angle Polarimeters -

Radiometers used to measure how the oscillation of sunlight within a geometric plane - known as its polarization - is changed by passing through clouds, aerosols, and the ocean. Measuring polarization states of UV-to-shortwave light at various angles provides detailed information on the atmosphere and ocean, such as particle size and composition

Combined, these instruments will be a major advance in satellite observing technology, allowing for new opportunities to monitor and respond to changes in our ecosystem, and the ways in which the atmosphere and ocean interact.

How will PACE advance beyond previous ocean color missions?

PACE's OCI instrument will be the most advanced for observing ocean color in NASA's history. Observing chlorophyll from space has a long heritage – the first ocean color satellite, NASA's Coastal Zone Color Scanner (CZCS) was launched in 1978. CZCS was a multi-spectral radiometer, meaning that it only collected data at a few specific wavelengths in the spectrum. As technology progressed, newer instruments have launched that sample additional wavelength bands, but they still don't reveal the entire story.

PACE represents a leap forward because it will view a very broad spectrum from 350 to 885 nanometer (nm) wavelengths at 5 nm intervals – making it hyperspectral. The range that PACE's instruments will observe includes the UV (350-400 nm), visible (400-700 nm), and near infrared (700-885 nm), as well as several shortwave infrared bands. Thus, the OCI will provide an unprecedented view of the ocean without the "blind spots" of previous sensors.

It is revealing to compare the OCI to a current ocean color sensor, such as the Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite (VIIRS) aboard Suomi National Polar-orbiting Partnership. Launched in 2011, VIIRS has been collecting data at five visible bands. The plot of VIIRS data shows individual data points representing example Harmful Algal Bloom (HAB) conditions in red and non-HAB conditions in black. In contrast, data on the PACE OCI plot is a much more complete picture, illustrating the nuances between the spectra of the two groups, aiding in their identification.
